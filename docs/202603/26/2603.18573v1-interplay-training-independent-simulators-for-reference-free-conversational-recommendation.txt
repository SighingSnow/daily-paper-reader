Abstract. Training conversational recommender systems (CRS) requires extensive dialogue data, which is challenging to collect at scale. To ad-dress this, researchers have used simulated user-recommender conversa-tions. Traditional simulation approaches often utilize a single large lan-guage model (LLM) that generates entire conversations with prior knowl-edge of the target items, leading to scripted and artificial dialogues. We propose a reference-free simulation framework that trains two indepen-dent LLMs, one as the user and one as the conversational recommender. These models interact in real-time without access to predetermined tar-get items, but preference summaries and target attributes, enabling the recommender to genuinely infer user preferences through dialogue. This approach produces more realistic and diverse conversations that closely mirror authentic human-AI interactions. Our reference-free simulators match or exceed existing methods in quality, while offering a scalable so-lution for generating high-quality conversational recommendation data without constraining conversations to pre-defined target items. We con-duct both quantitative and human evaluations to confirm the effective-ness of our reference-free approach. 

# 1 Introduction 

Conversational Recommender Systems (CRS) are mixed-initiative dialogue sys-tems that offer personalized, context-sensitive recommendations through natu-ral language conversations [22]. Unlike traditional systems that rely on static user-item interactions, CRS allow dynamic, interactive feedback from users and enable both parties to guide the conversation. The development of CRS depends on rich conversational data, but collecting human annotated conversations is costly and limited in quality. Previous work highlights key issues in crowd-sourced CRS datasets: (1) Generic utterances :Due to lack of genuine preferences, workers often say vague statements like “I like most genres,” limiting personalization [12]; (2) Lack of context : Qiu et al. showed that crowd-sourced conversations often lack depth and context, making it difficult for CRS to accurately model user preferences and provide relevant suggestions; and (3) Limited domain expertise : Qiu et al. [20] noted that crowd-workers often lack domain expertise, resulting in weak recommendations and poor explanations. Joko et al. [10] further showed that even well-designed crowd data lacks multi-session realism and is hard to scale without sacrificing authenticity. Human-written conversations are also costly and labor-intensive to collect at scale. The growing popularity of large language models (LLMs) has led to the use of LLM simulators to generate authentic and context-rich conversations that are critical for training CRS. Zhou et al. [30] found that topic-guided dialogues improve recommendations, underscoring the need for synthetic data that mimic natural interactions. Recent approaches use LLMs like GPT-4o to generate CRS conversations [12, 17, 31], but generally rely on reference-dependent generation, feeding target items to simulators in advance. This causes: (1) data leakage (the simulators knows the answer beforehand) and (2) rigid dialogues (exploration is limited, reducing realism). Additionally, using general-purpose LLMs as static role-players for both conversational parties is problematic [13]. These models are designed as helpful assistants rather than realistic user simulators with diverse and sometimes inconsistent preferences. The resulting conversations often lack the natural exploration, uncertainty, and flexibility that characterize genuine human-recommender interactions.

The user simulator operates with preference summaries and attribute descriptions, providing realistic feed-back without knowing specific targets. The recommender simulator generates contextually appropriate recommendations based solely on the evolving conver-sation. This design enables genuine exploration where recommendations emerge naturally from dialogue rather than predetermined scripts. Our approach offers several advantages over existing methods. The reference-free design eliminates data leakage by ensuring that simulators discover infor-mation through conversation rather than having it predetermined for them. In-dependent training of specialized models creates more realistic user and recom-mender behaviors than using general-purpose LLMs for both roles. The inter-active nature allows flexible conversations that can naturally evolve in multiple directions, better reflecting the complexity of real recommendation scenarios. Through comprehensive evaluations, we demonstrate that our simulators gener-ate conversations comparable in quality to existing approaches while addressing the methodological limitations that have constrained previous work. The result-ing synthetic dialogues exhibit natural exploration patterns, genuine uncertainty, and flexibility that characterize human conversations. We make the following contributions in this chapter: 1. We propose a reference-free simulation framework that removes the oracle problem by training independent user and recommender simulators without access to the target item, allowing for the exploration of realistic preferences and flexible recommendation behavior. 2. We show that simulators trained on target attributes, not items, outperform larger general models: Our 8B recommender achieves 15 × higher accuracy than 32B baselines while preserving strong response diversity. 3. # 2 Related Works 

Conversational Recommender Systems. CRS integrate dialogue interfaces with personalized recommendation engines, allowing users to request and receive item suggestions through natural language interaction [7, 22]. Unlike one-shot recommenders, CRS conduct multi-turn conversations to elicit user preferences, clarify requirements, and offer explanations [7]. Recent advances have shifted from modular pipelines [11] to end-to-end models [15] and prompt-based ap-proaches that achieve strong zero-shot performance [4, 18]. However, these sys-tems face challenges in preference modeling, with methods such as CORAL that use LLMs to handle contrasting user preferences [14]. 

User Simulators. User simulators enable scalable CRS training and evaluation without human participants [28]. Early simulator techniques were rule-based but 4 Ramos et al. 

too rigid [11], while recent approaches leverage LLMs with persona descriptions for more realistic responses. However, a critical limitation is reference depen-dency: many methods provide simulators with target items, creating artificial constraints [27]. Frameworks like CSHI balance realism and consistency through human-in-the-loop guidance [32]. Our work addresses these issues through inde-pendent simulator training. 

Dialogue Data Collection and Generation. CRS research faces a trade-off between data quality and scalability. Human-centric methods produce high quality but are expensive [28], while crowdsourcing offers scale but suffers from generic responses and quality issues [12]. LLM-based generation has emerged as a solution, using techniques such as template-driven approaches [17] and ac-tive synthesis [23]. However, maintaining reference-independence while ensuring diversity remains challenging. 

Interactive Simulation and Evaluation Challenges. Recent multi-agent approaches in CRS use independent user and recommender simulators to foster natural interactions. Methods like MACRS [3] and Collaborative Self-Play [2] leverage multiple LLMs with distinct roles, while reinforcement learning-based systems such as CRSIRL [1] and AMIE [25] explore self-play and intrinsic re-ward modeling. However, true agent independence remains unexplored. Simul-taneously, evaluation challenges persist: datasets like ReDial [16] and Open-DialKG [19] suffer from data leakage, inflating performance through exposed targets [27]. Newer methods, such as FACE [9], aim to assess the quality of the dialogue without references, addressing the disconnect between traditional metrics and human judgment [28] and highlighting the need for reference-free, target-agnostic evaluation. 

# 3 Methodology 

We propose Interplay – a framework that trains independent user and recom-mender simulators to interact without predetermined outcomes, allowing genuine exploration and flexible conversation dynamics that mirror real-world recommen-dation scenarios. 

3.1 The Core Insight 

The core challenge in simulating conversational recommendation lies in replicat-ing the natural tension of real interactions: users bring preferences but stay open to discovering unexpected options, while recommenders must explore needs with-out knowing what will click. This tension is lost when a single model plays both roles with shared knowledge. Our approach addresses this challenge through two key innovations. First, we train completely independent simulators, each opti-mized solely for its respective role, ensuring that natural behavioral differences emerge rather than being artificially programmed. Second, we remove reference dependence by using target attributes instead of items, prompting simulators to engage in genuine discovery over scripted responses. 3.2 Breaking the Single-Target Constraint 

Prior user simulation methods are fundamentally rigid – they condition responses on fixed target movies, making simulated users reject strong recommendations simply because they do not match a preset title [27]. This creates an artificial constraint that real users never face. When someone seeks “a romantic com-edy for date night,” they usually remain open to various films that meet those criteria rather than focusing on a single predetermined choice. Our user simu-lator captures natural flexibility by reasoning over target attributes instead of fixed items. Rather than knowing “that the user wants Inception,” the simulator is trained to understand preferences like “a mind-bending thriller with complex plotting.” This change allows the user simulator to accept a range of valid recom-mendations, freeing conversations from the constraint of converging on a single outcome. As a result, dialogues unfold more organically, driven by authentic attribute alignment, rather than scripted goals. 

3.3 Persona Architecture for Authentic User Behavior 

To produce realistic interactions, our user simulator integrates three persona components that capture diverse aspects of user context while preserving flex-ibility. The general preferences component offers a natural language summary of the user’s broad tastes, generated by analyzing a subset of their viewing his-tory. This summary grounds the simulator in a consistent personality across conversations while still allowing variation in specific choices. The movie his-tory component includes three previously watched movies with corresponding reviews, randomly sampled from the user’s profile to provide concrete examples of their tastes. These serve as conversational anchors, allowing the recommender to reference and build on specific, discussable preferences. The three random movies were originally sampled with the PEARL dataset and we reuse the same movies in order to maintain the same context and avoid data leakage. The target attributes component then introduces preference cues extracted from a target movie’s review, without revealing the movie title. This preserves authentic user intent while removing reference dependence: the simulator knows what qualities to look for, but not which item should satisfy them. This architecture enables our user simulator to maintain a consistent personality while remaining genuinely open to discovery, accurately mirroring how real users approach recommendation conversations with both clear preferences and openness to surprise.

Our recommender simulator learns to handle preference elicitation and rec-ommendation generation through fluid conversations, adapting dynamically to evolving user needs rather than following rigid patterns. 3.5 Independent Training for Specialized Behavior 

Our novel training strategy is composed of two key components designed to foster specialized agent behavior. First, we enforce a structured output format to make conversational actions explicit. Second, we employ a role-specific loss masking technique to ensure each simulator learns exclusively from its own turns. 

Structured Action Generation To enforce role-specific behavior, we struc-ture the entire model output to separate the intended action from the natu-ral language response . Each generated turn adheres to a strict format where an 

<action> block contains a specific command: <recommend> , <inquire> , <greeting> ,

<disclose-goal> , <feedback> , and <accept> . Afterwards, a <response> block provides the user-facing text, including the movie title, where applicable. These structural tokens are added to the tokenizer before training. This design provides two key advantages: Interplay : Training Independent Simulators for Reference-Free CRS 7

1. Structured Reasoning: It imposes an explicit response template, teaching the model to first commit to an unambiguous action before generating its utterance. For example, when providing a recommendation, the model can respond with: 

<action><recommend></action><response> You may enjoy <movie_title>Good Will Hunting (1997)</movie_title> .2. Simplified Analysis: The structured output format enables reliable post-simulation parsing, allowing us to programmatically extract and analyze the sequence of actions and recommended items. Crucially, the user simulator is trained to output <accept> when it encounters a satisfactory recommen-dation. This provides a natural and concrete stopping signal, even if the original oracle item is never recommended. In contrast, oracle-based meth-ods can only terminate once the ground-truth item is recommended, often leading to either excessively long conversations or manual intervention where the ground truth is injected after a fixed number of turns — an unrealis-tic shortcut. Our reference-free design avoids this rigidity: conversations can conclude flexibly as soon as user satisfaction is reached. While the possibil-ity of indefinitely extended dialogues still exists in principle, in practice this approach greatly reduces the need for manual intervention and more closely reflects the open-ended nature of real human–recommender interactions.

3.6 Emergent Realistic Dynamics 

When our independently trained simulators interact, their distinct objectives and perspectives produce authentic conversational dynamics that single-model setups fail to capture. The user simulator enters with clear yet flexible goals, ex-pressing preferences naturally and staying open to unexpected but fitting sugges-tions. Meanwhile, the recommender must genuinely interpret these preferences, asking clarifying questions and generating recommendations based on the evolv-ing dialogue, not preloaded answers. This independence is key to realism: the natural tension between user uncertainty and recommender confidence emerges organically from their interaction. 

# 4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 Experimental Details 

We perform preprocessing steps to filter PEARL [12]. Specifically, we removed turns with empty dialogues and removed conversations with movies that were not part of the IMDB dataset used to generate PEARL . We filtered 7.65% of the original dataset, leaving our final dataset with 52.9k conversations. We fine-tune our both simulators using Llama 3.1 8B [5] and Qwen 3 8B [24]. Following Kong et al. [13], we use the base model versions rather than the instruct version to avoid the issue of pre-existing system alignment. We perform full fine-tuning on both models for 2 epochs on 4 Nvidia A100 GPUs with a batch size of 4, gradient accumulation steps of 4, and a learning rate of 2e-4. We evaluate on the filtered test set of the PEARL dataset, containing 2,069 conversations with seen user personas but unseen target items. For full imple-mentation details, see: https://github.com/jeromeramos70/interplay-sim .

4.2 Baselines 

Although previous works use closed-sourced models GPT3.5 or GPT4 to simulate conversations, we rely only on open-source baselines for reproducibility. Namely, we use the instruction-tuned versions of Llama3.1 70B [5] and Qwen3 32B [24] as zero-shot user and recommender simulators. Furthermore, we fine-tune Uni-CRS [26], a popular CRS model, on the PEARL dataset as another baseline. For our zero-shot baselines, we construct prompts that mirror the input to our fine-tuned models. For the user simulator role, the prompt includes the user’s persona (general preferences, movie history with reviews) and the target attributes. For the recommender simulator role, the prompt includes the user’s persona and the full conversation history. This setup ensures a fair comparison by providing the baselines with the same contextual information as our models. 

4.3 Evaluation Metrics 

We assess our simulators with automatic metrics on two fronts: (i) multi-turn simulation quality and (ii) single-turn response quality . Human evaluations are described in Section 5.3. Interplay : Training Independent Simulators for Reference-Free CRS 9

User Simulation Quality Each test dialogue has a known ground-truth target item. As the recommender proposes items, the simulator either accepts or rejects them. We summarize outcomes with three categories: 

– Success Rate (SR): The simulator accepts the ground-truth item. 

– Early Termination (ET): The simulator accepts some non–ground-truth 

item before the ground-truth is even proposed. 

– Failure Rate (FR): The simulator rejects all recommendations, including the ground-truth item. Higher SR and lower FR indicate that the simulator reliably follows user goals and accepts valid recommendations. A moderate ET rate can also be desirable: it reflects that the simulator occasionally accepts alternative items instead of always defaulting to the ground-truth recommendation, capturing a more di-verse and realistic range of user preferences. By definition, these three rates are mutually exclusive and together account for 100% of dialogues. 

Single-Turn Response Quality To evaluate the quality of individual user responses, we report: 

– BERTScore: We measure the semantic similarity between the generated user responses and the ground-truth responses at each turn to evaluate con-textual relevance (higher is better) [29]. 

– Dist-4: Measures lexical diversity as the fraction of unique 4-grams in the generated responses, computed over the whole test set (higher is better). 

Recommender Performance In addition to Dist-4, we report the following metrics for the recommender: 

– Recall@1: We check if the target item in a given turn is correct because our model is trained to only output a single recommendation in a single turn (higher is better). 

– Match Score: To better evaluate recommendation quality in a reference-free context, we introduce Match Score. This metric quantifies the average similarity between the recommended items and the ground-truth movie. The score is the cosine similarity between movie embeddings from a model trained on the MovieLens-32M dataset [6]. This model uses TinyBERT [8] to encode semantic features (e.g., title, tags, genres) and learns interaction relevance via BPR loss [21] to capture user preferences (higher is better).

Evaluators found that our reference-free dialogues exhibited significantly more user control, though they did not match the naturalness of PEARL . This gap is partly attributable to how the models are trained: because our simulators are trained on PEARL ’s LLM-generated text, they may inherit its stylistic patterns, so perceived naturalness likely reflects the training data’s style rather than a limitation of our reference-free design. # 6 Limitations 

Domain Specificity Our empirical evaluation is confined to the movie rec-ommendation domain, chosen due to the availability of large-scale, high-quality public datasets like PEARL [12]. While the Interplay framework is designed to be domain-agnostic, its effectiveness in other domains such as e-commerce, mu-sic, or travel has not yet been validated. Future work should focus on applying and evaluating our independent simulator approach in these different contexts, which may present unique challenges like different conversational dynamics. Interplay : Training Independent Simulators for Reference-Free CRS 13 

Constrained Conversational Scope The current simulation operates within a structured, task-oriented dialogue framework where conversational turns are mapped to explicit actions like <recommend> or <feedback> . This approach is beneficial for control and analysis but does not capture the full spectrum of real-world interactions. Human conversations often involve open-domain chit-chat, multi-intent utterances (e.g., asking a question and providing feedback in the same turn), and more fluid topic shifts. A key direction for future research is to enhance the simulators to handle these less structured, mixed-initiative conversations. 

Limitation on Recommendation Evaluation Although MatchScore is a useful automatic proxy for recommendation quality, it has not yet been val-idated as a reliable metric that aligns with real user preferences. Future work should include user studies to evaluate whether automatic metrics such as Match-Score correlates with human judgments, and more broadly evaluate whether the reference-free simulation results reflect real-world user behavior. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We tackle the oracle problem in conversational recommender system simula-tion, where access to target items creates unrealistic, constrained interactions. Our reference-free framework trains independent user and recommender sim-ulators without predetermined outcomes, enabling more natural conversations and flexible recommendation behavior. By focusing on target attributes rather than items, user simulators accept multiple valid suggestions, while recommender simulators develop genuine preference understanding without relying on oracle knowledge. Our approach outperforms larger models across key metrics: user simulators achieve 93–95% success rates without target leakage, and our 8B recommenders surpass 32B baselines by 15 × in accuracy while maintaining high diversity. Hu-man evaluations confirm our dialogues match reference-dependent quality, but with far lower computational cost. This establishes a scalable, realistic simulation paradigm for CRS and paves the way for more advanced multi-agent systems in conversational AI.
